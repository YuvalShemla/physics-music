# Waves, Particles, and Sound

**A Virtual Exhibition Tracing How Physics Discoveries Shaped Western Music, 1860s--2020s**

*Subtitle/Tagline:* **One hundred sixty years of collisions between laboratory and concert hall.**

---

## Entrance Text

Every sound you have ever heard is a physics experiment. A vibrating string obeys the same equations that describe light crossing the universe. A concert hall is a laboratory of resonant frequencies. And for over a century and a half, composers have known it.

This exhibition traces ten collisions between physics and Western music -- from Hermann von Helmholtz measuring overtones in 1863 to a quantum computer improvising alongside a violinist in 2023. Some connections are direct: a composer reads a physics paper and writes a score from its equations. Others are cultural: a discovery rewrites the rules of reality, and artists respond in parallel, without ever meeting the scientists. We mark each connection honestly. What remains constant is the pattern: every time physics expands what we know about sound, space, time, or matter, composers expand what counts as music.

Walk the arc. Press play. Listen for the physics inside the sound.

## Station 1: The Anatomy of a Single Tone

**Date range displayed:** 1863 / 1894

### Physics Panel

In 1863, the German physicist Hermann von Helmholtz published *On the Sensations of Tone*, a book that changed how humanity understood sound. Using ingeniously simple equipment -- tuning forks, glass resonators, and the human ear -- Helmholtz proved that every musical tone is not a single thing but a *composite*. When you hear a clarinet play middle C, you are actually hearing dozens of frequencies sounding simultaneously: a fundamental pitch plus a ladder of overtones rising above it. The specific recipe of overtones -- which ones are strong, which are faint -- is what gives each instrument its unique color, its *timbre*. Helmholtz had revealed the hidden architecture inside every note.

### Music Panel

In 1894, Claude Debussy premiered *Prelude a l'apres-midi d'un faune* in Paris, and the trajectory of Western music shifted. The piece opens with an unaccompanied flute solo -- a sinuous chromatic melody that floats free of any clear key center. When the orchestra enters, Debussy layers translucent washes of harp, horn, and strings that shimmer and dissolve rather than march through conventional chord progressions. The texture is luminous and weightless. Instead of building tension and resolving it (the engine of Romantic harmony), Debussy treats chords as pure *sonorities* -- blocks of color valued for the way they sound in the moment, not for where they lead.

### Connection Panel

Helmholtz demonstrated that timbre -- the color of a sound -- arises from the overtone series, a physical structure hidden inside every note. Debussy, who studied acoustics at the Paris Conservatoire and knew Helmholtz's work, composed with that structure in mind. His parallel chord movement and avoidance of functional resolution treat harmony as a palette of sonic colors rather than a grammar of tension and release. The connection is *informed influence*: Debussy did not illustrate Helmholtz, but Helmholtz gave him permission to hear chords as textures rather than functions.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play to hear the opening flute solo, and notice how the melody seems to belong to no key at all.

### Color/Mood Note

Warm amber and soft gold lighting. The mood is late-afternoon impressionism -- hazy, sensuous, suspended in time.

## Station 2: Music from Thin Air

**Date range displayed:** 1920

### Physics Panel

In 1920, a young Russian physicist named Lev Sergeyevich Termen -- known in the West as Leon Theremin -- was researching high-frequency electrical circuits in a Petrograd laboratory. He noticed something uncanny: when he moved his hand near an antenna connected to an oscillator circuit, the pitch of the audible tone changed. The principle was *heterodyne oscillation* -- two high-frequency electromagnetic signals beating against each other to produce an audible difference tone. His body's capacitance, the ability of a conductor to store electrical charge, altered one of the two frequencies. The closer his hand, the higher the pitch. He had stumbled upon the first instrument you play without touching.

### Music Panel

The theremin found its greatest interpreter in Clara Rockmore, a Lithuanian-born violinist whose small hands ended her string career but whose preternatural pitch control made her the instrument's undisputed virtuoso. Rockmore performed concert transcriptions of Saint-Saens, Rachmaninoff, and Tchaikovsky, coaxing from the theremin a tone that was part cello, part soprano voice -- a continuous, vibrato-rich legato unlike anything else in music. Her right hand sculpted pitch in the air; her left controlled volume. The result was eerie, lyrical, and heartbreakingly expressive: proof that an electromagnetic field could sing.

### Connection Panel

This is the most literal physics-to-music link in the exhibition. The theremin does not *use* electromagnetic principles as metaphor -- it *is* an electromagnetic instrument. The performer's body becomes a variable capacitor in a circuit. Every gesture changes the electric field, and every change in the field becomes audible pitch or volume. Leon Theremin was a physicist first and an instrument builder second. The theremin is physics made musical, with no intermediary.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play to hear Clara Rockmore perform "The Swan" by Saint-Saens, and watch the animation showing how the electromagnetic field shifts with her hand.

### Color/Mood Note

Electric violet and deep indigo. Faint crackling static animations on the walls. The mood is nocturnal, mysterious, and otherworldly.

## Station 3: Simultaneous Perspectives

**Date range displayed:** 1905 / 1913

### Physics Panel

In 1905, a twenty-six-year-old patent clerk in Bern named Albert Einstein published his theory of Special Relativity. Its central claim was staggering: there is no single, absolute frame of reference in the universe. Two observers moving at different speeds will measure different lengths, different durations, even different sequences of events -- and both will be correct. Time itself is relative. Simultaneity is an illusion. The comfortable Newtonian world, in which space and time were fixed and universal, cracked open. In the same decade, Pablo Picasso shattered the single viewpoint in painting, showing multiple angles of a face on one canvas. Reality, it seemed, depended on where you stood.

### Music Panel

On May 29, 1913, the Theatre des Champs-Elysees in Paris erupted into one of the most famous riots in art history. The cause: Igor Stravinsky's *The Rite of Spring*. From the opening bassoon solo -- pitched unnervingly high -- the ballet assaulted every expectation. Stravinsky stacked rhythms that refuse to align (*polyrhythm*), layered keys that clash (*polytonality*), and drove pounding, asymmetric accents that made the audience's bodies unable to find a downbeat. The texture is dense, violent, and kaleidoscopic. Multiple musical "realities" coexist simultaneously, each with its own pulse, its own tonal center, its own momentum.

### Connection Panel

Einstein, Picasso, and Stravinsky never collaborated, and no direct influence connects them. But the cultural parallel is striking and widely noted by historians: within a single decade (1905--1913), physics, visual art, and music each dismantled the idea of a single, stable perspective. Relativity denied absolute time; Cubism denied a single viewpoint; *The Rite of Spring* denied a single meter and key. The connection is *synchronous rupture* -- three disciplines independently concluding that reality is plural.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play to hear the "Sacrificial Dance" finale, and try to count along -- you will find more than one pulse competing for your attention.

### Color/Mood Note

Fractured red and burnt orange, with sharp geometric projections on the walls evoking Cubist planes. The mood is explosive, angular, and restless.

## Station 4: Sound After the Unthinkable

**Date range displayed:** 1945 / 1960

### Physics Panel

On July 16, 1945, in the New Mexico desert, physicists detonated the first nuclear weapon -- the Trinity test. The bomb's power came from nuclear fission: splitting the nuclei of uranium or plutonium atoms to release the energy that binds protons and neutrons together. The energy released was equivalent to 21,000 tons of TNT. Weeks later, bombs fell on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, killing over 200,000 people. J. Robert Oppenheimer, the project's scientific director, famously recalled a line from the Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." Physics had produced the most terrifying object in human history.

### Music Panel

In 1960, the Polish composer Krzysztof Penderecki completed *Threnody for the Victims of Hiroshima* for 52 string instruments. The piece uses no melody and no harmony in any traditional sense. Instead, Penderecki invented a new notation -- dense clusters of graphic symbols on the page -- to direct players to produce *tone clusters* (massive blocks of adjacent pitches), *col legno* strikes (hitting the strings with the wood of the bow), and unearthly glissandi that slide through quarter-tones. The sound is a scream. The texture is a wall of anguished noise that tightens, shrieks, and finally fades to silence. It lasts just over eight minutes.

### Connection Panel

The connection here is *moral response*, not technical transfer. Penderecki did not use nuclear physics in his compositional method. He used music to bear witness to what nuclear physics had made possible. His graphic score, however, visually resembles the trajectory diagrams of particle physics -- dense, branching lines that evoke the paths of subatomic particles in a cloud chamber. Whether intentional or not, the visual echo is haunting. The piece asks: what does music sound like after physics has destroyed a city?

### Visitor Instruction

Press play and close your eyes. The piece is eight minutes long; let the sound surround you completely.

### Color/Mood Note

Ashen white fading to charcoal black. No warm colors. A single harsh overhead light. The mood is grief, shock, and the silence after detonation.

## Station 5: The Sound of Nothing

**Date range displayed:** 1927 / 1952

### Physics Panel

In 1927, Werner Heisenberg formulated the uncertainty principle, and quantum mechanics revealed something philosophers had debated for millennia: perfect emptiness does not exist. Even in a vacuum -- a region with no matter, no light, no radiation -- quantum field theory predicts ceaseless activity. Virtual particles flicker into existence and annihilate each other on timescales too brief to observe directly, but their effects are measurable (the Casimir effect, the Lamb shift). The quantum vacuum *seethes*. "Nothing" is not nothing. Empty space has structure, energy, and fluctuation. Silence, at the quantum level, is an impossibility.

### Music Panel

In 1951, composer John Cage visited the anechoic chamber at Harvard University -- a room engineered to absorb all reflected sound. He expected to hear silence. Instead, he heard two sounds: a high-pitched tone (his nervous system) and a low hum (his blood circulating). Silence was unreachable. In 1952, Cage premiered *4'33"*: a work in three movements during which the performer plays nothing. The "music" is whatever sounds occur -- coughing, wind, shuffling, a distant car. The piece redefines listening. Every performance is unique because every room, every audience, every moment is different.

### Connection Panel

Physicist Donald Spector published a peer-reviewed paper in 2015 arguing that *4'33"* is the auditory analogue of the quantum vacuum: in both cases, the absence of deliberate signal reveals that "empty" space is full of activity. Cage himself did not cite quantum mechanics, but his anechoic chamber revelation -- that silence does not exist -- mirrors the quantum discovery that the vacuum is never truly empty. The parallel is *structural*: both physics and music arrived independently at the same insight about the impossibility of nothing.

### Visitor Instruction

Sit in the quiet alcove for thirty seconds. Do not press play. Listen to what you hear. That is the piece.

### Color/Mood Note

Pure white walls with the faintest gray gradient. Near-silence in the space. The mood is contemplative, startling in its stillness, unexpectedly alive.

## Station 6: Sound in Motion

**Date range displayed:** 1842 / 1908

### Physics Panel

In 1842, the Austrian mathematician Christian Doppler proposed that the observed frequency of a wave changes when the source and the observer move relative to each other. You hear it every day: an ambulance siren rises in pitch as it approaches and drops as it recedes. The sound waves compress in front of the moving source (higher frequency) and stretch behind it (lower frequency). Doppler published his theory using the motion of stars, but it was first tested with sound in 1845, when the Dutch meteorologist Christophorus Buys Ballot placed trumpeters on a moving train near Utrecht and had musicians with perfect pitch stand along the tracks, noting the change.

### Music Panel

Charles Ives grew up in Danbury, Connecticut, with a father who ran extraordinary sonic experiments. George Ives would position two marching bands on opposite sides of town and have his son stand in the middle, listening as the bands approached and receded -- each playing different music in different keys and tempos. From this childhood, Ives composed *The Unanswered Question* (1908): a work for three separate groups of musicians placed apart in space. A slow, sustained string chorale floats serenely; a solo trumpet repeatedly poses a five-note phrase; a woodwind quartet answers with increasingly frantic, atonal outbursts. The three layers exist in different musical worlds, overlapping but never converging.

### Connection Panel

Ives' father was not teaching him the Doppler effect by name, but the experiment was Doppler's principle in action: sound changes with spatial motion and relative position. Ives absorbed this into his compositional DNA. His spatial separation of ensembles, his layering of independent tempos, and his embrace of what an audience hears from a specific position in space all connect to the physics of sound in motion. The link is *experiential and instrumental* -- Ives composed from the listener's moving body, not from the physicist's equation.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play and notice the three independent layers -- strings, trumpet, woodwinds -- each living in its own space and tempo.

### Color/Mood Note

Twilight blue deepening to night sky. Sounds emanate from three different directions in the station. The mood is questioning, vast, and quietly unsettled.

## Station 7: Music by Probability

**Date range displayed:** 1860s / 1955--56

### Physics Panel

In the 1860s and 1870s, James Clerk Maxwell and Ludwig Boltzmann developed statistical mechanics -- a revolutionary framework for understanding systems with vast numbers of particles. Instead of tracking each individual gas molecule (an impossibility), they described the collective behavior of billions of molecules using probability distributions. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution predicts how molecular speeds are spread across a population at a given temperature. Brownian motion -- the jittery, random path of a pollen grain buffeted by invisible molecules -- became a visible signature of this statistical reality. Order emerges from randomness. A cloud of gas has temperature and pressure not because individual molecules are coordinated, but because probability governs the aggregate.

### Music Panel

Iannis Xenakis was a Greek-Romanian composer who trained as an engineer and architect under Le Corbusier before turning to music. In *Pithoprakta* (1955--56), scored for 46 string instruments, two trombones, xylophone, and woodblock, Xenakis applied statistical mechanics directly. Each string player performs an independent glissando -- a continuous slide between pitches -- with the pitch, duration, speed, and direction determined by probability equations drawn from the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and the theory of Brownian motion. No two parts are alike. The result is a seething, swarming mass of sound that coheres not through melody or harmony but through *statistical density* -- the sonic equivalent of a gas cloud.

### Connection Panel

This is *explicit, mathematical application*. Xenakis did not use physics as metaphor; he imported the equations directly into his compositional method and published them in his 1971 book *Formalized Music*. Each glissando in *Pithoprakta* follows a probability curve derived from kinetic theory. The orchestra becomes a gas: 46 independent particles whose collective motion produces emergent form. Among the ten stations in this exhibition, this is one of the most rigorous science-to-score translations.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play and watch the animated score: each glissando line represents one player-particle moving through pitch-space according to probability.

### Color/Mood Note

Deep charcoal with thousands of tiny white particle-lights drifting slowly across the walls. The mood is vast, abstract, and strangely organic -- like watching weather from space.

## Station 8: The Room Reveals Itself

**Date range displayed:** 1969

### Physics Panel

Every enclosed space has resonant frequencies -- specific pitches at which sound waves reflect off the walls, floor, and ceiling in patterns that reinforce themselves, creating *standing waves*. These frequencies are determined by the room's dimensions, shape, and materials. If you sustain a tone at one of these frequencies, the room amplifies it; at other frequencies, the room absorbs or scatters the energy. This is the physics of *acoustic filtering*: the room acts as a filter, strengthening some frequencies and suppressing others. Architectural acousticians have studied these properties since Wallace Clement Sabine's pioneering measurements at Harvard in the 1890s. Every room has a sonic fingerprint.

### Music Panel

In 1969, the American composer Alvin Lucier sat in a room with a microphone and a tape recorder. He recorded himself speaking a simple text: "I am sitting in a room different from the one you are in now." He played the recording back into the room through a loudspeaker and re-recorded it. Then he played *that* recording back and re-recorded again. And again. And again. Over 32 iterations, his voice gradually dissolves. The consonants blur first, then the vowels smear, and finally all speech vanishes, replaced by pure, shimmering tones -- the resonant frequencies of the room itself. The words are gone. The room is singing.

### Connection Panel

The composition *is* the physics. There is no metaphor, no translation, no abstraction. Lucier designed a process that physically demonstrates acoustic resonance and filtering. Each re-recording cycle strips away frequencies the room does not reinforce and amplifies those it does. The room becomes the instrument, the score, and the performer. Of all ten stations, this is the case where the boundary between physics demonstration and musical composition disappears entirely.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play and listen for the moment when you can no longer understand the words -- that is the room taking over.

### Color/Mood Note

Warm concrete gray with subtle golden resonance rings rippling outward on the walls when the playback begins. The mood is meditative, architectural, and gradually transcendent.

## Station 9: Orchestrating the Spectrogram

**Date range displayed:** 1822 / 1975

### Physics Panel

In 1822, the French mathematician Joseph Fourier proved one of the most powerful results in the history of science: any complex waveform, no matter how irregular, can be decomposed into a sum of simple sine waves of different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases. This is *Fourier analysis*. Applied to sound, it means that any musical tone -- a trombone blast, a human vowel, a cymbal crash -- can be broken into its component frequencies and displayed as a *spectrogram*: a visual map showing which frequencies are present and how strong each one is. By the 1970s, computers at institutions like IRCAM in Paris could perform this analysis in real time, making the hidden structure of sound visible on a screen.

### Music Panel

In 1975, the French composer Gerard Grisey stood at IRCAM and recorded a low E played on a trombone. He fed the sound into a spectrograph and studied the printout: a tower of overtones rising above the fundamental, each with a specific frequency and amplitude. Then he did something no composer had done so precisely before: he *orchestrated the spectrogram*. Each overtone was assigned to a different instrument in the ensemble -- the fundamental to the trombone, the second partial to the horn, the third to the clarinet, and so on upward through the orchestra. The resulting piece, *Partiels*, begins as a single trombone note and slowly blooms into a shimmering, iridescent chord that is literally the inside of that note made audible.

### Connection Panel

This is the most direct physics-to-music translation in the exhibition. Grisey did not interpret Fourier analysis or use it as inspiration -- he fed a sound into a machine, read the data, and transcribed the data onto orchestral staves. The ensemble *plays* a spectrogram. The school of composition that emerged, *Spectral Music*, treats Fourier's theorem not as a metaphor but as a compositional method. The orchestra becomes a loudspeaker reconstructing a sound from its frequency components.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play, and watch the spectrogram on screen as the orchestra unfolds the overtones of a single trombone note in real time.

### Color/Mood Note

Deep ocean blue at the base rising through turquoise, green, and gold at the top -- the color map of a spectrogram. The mood is revelatory, luminous, and slowly expanding.

## Station 10: Quantum Duet

**Date range displayed:** 2023

### Physics Panel

A quantum computer does not process information the way your laptop does. Instead of classical bits (which are either 0 or 1), it uses *qubits* -- quantum bits that can exist in a *superposition* of both states simultaneously. When multiple qubits are entangled, their states become correlated in ways that have no classical analogue. Only when you *measure* a qubit does it collapse from superposition into a definite value -- and the outcome is fundamentally probabilistic. In 2022, IBM unveiled the Eagle processor with 127 qubits. By 2023, quantum computers were powerful enough to run circuits whose outputs could serve as the basis for creative decisions -- including musical ones.

### Music Panel

In 2023, the composer and computer scientist Eduardo Reck Miranda premiered *Qubism* at the Edinburgh Science Festival. The piece is structured as a call-and-response duet between a live violinist and an IBM quantum computer. The violinist plays a phrase; the quantum computer processes it through a quantum circuit, and the probabilistic output of qubit measurements determines the responding phrase -- its pitches, rhythms, and durations. No two performances are identical, because quantum measurement is inherently non-deterministic. The violin tone is warm, human, and familiar; the quantum responses are unpredictable yet musically coherent, shaped by the composer's careful circuit design. The ancient form of call-and-response meets the newest technology on Earth.

### Connection Panel

The 160-year arc of this exhibition closes here. Helmholtz used tuning forks; Miranda uses qubits. But the pattern holds: a physics discovery expands the space of possible sounds, and a composer steps into that space. In *Qubism*, quantum computation does not merely inspire music -- it *generates* musical decisions through measurement and superposition. The responding "performer" is a quantum computer. The connection is *generative and direct*: the physics is not metaphor but collaborator.

### Visitor Instruction

Press play to hear a live-recorded performance, and watch the quantum circuit diagram update in real time as each qubit measurement triggers the violin's response.

### Color/Mood Note

Black walls with luminous circuit-diagram lines in cool cyan and magenta, pulsing gently with each qubit measurement. The mood is futuristic, intimate, and full of wonder -- the endpoint of a long arc, opening onto what comes next.

## Exit Text

You have walked 160 years in ten stations -- from Helmholtz's tuning forks to IBM's qubits. The pattern repeats: physics reveals something new about the nature of sound, matter, space, or time, and composers respond by rewriting the rules of music. Sometimes the influence is direct (a spectrogram transcribed onto orchestral staves). Sometimes it is a cultural echo (relativity and polyrhythm shattering certainty in the same decade). The boundary between science and art is more porous than either side usually admits.

What comes next? Artificial intelligence is already composing. Gravitational wave detectors are converting the collisions of black holes into audible sound. The next station in this exhibition has not been built yet -- but it will be. Listen for it.
